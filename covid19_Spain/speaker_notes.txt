SPEAKER NOTES — COVID-19 in Spain (DAV 2026)
~10 minutes total | 19 slides | ~30 seconds per slide
============================================================


SLIDE 1 — Title
---------------
Our project analyzes COVID-19 in Spain, with Poland as a comparison throughout.
Spain was one of the hardest hit countries in Europe in the first wave, but also
had one of the fastest vaccination campaigns and a distinct regional structure.
We'll cover descriptive trends, regional heterogeneity, economics, and inferential
models — roughly 30 seconds per slide.


SLIDE 2 — Daily cases
---------------------
Spain's first wave hit hard and fast — weeks before Poland saw significant spread.
Spain's waves are sharper overall, reflecting denser cities and early international
travel. Poland's tend to be flatter but longer. Both went through roughly five to
six waves, but timing and scale differ sharply.


SLIDE 3 — Daily deaths
----------------------
Spain's April 2020 peak was one of the highest in Europe per capita — over 120,000
official deaths by end of 2024, at 342 per 100,000 population. The vertical lines
mark policy events — deaths fell a few weeks after the state of alarm. From 2021
the peaks flatten with vaccination. Poland's per-capita toll was actually higher
in the later waves.


SLIDE 4 — Excess mortality vs reported deaths
---------------------------------------------
This compares official COVID deaths to excess mortality — deaths above what we'd
expect in a normal year. In spring 2020 the gap is large: many deaths in care homes
or at home were never attributed to COVID. Official early counts are a clear
underestimate. This is one of the most important caveats when reading raw numbers.


SLIDE 5 — Test positivity rate
------------------------------
WHO considers positivity above 5% a sign of insufficient testing. Spain hit 12.5%
in spring 2020, meaning official case counts that period can't be taken at face
value. When positivity is high, the real incidence is a multiple of reported cases.


SLIDE 6 — Vaccination (animated)
---------------------------------
Press play if it hasn't started. Spain reached ~183 doses per 100 people — among
the highest in Europe. The rollout was faster than Poland's from the start.
The drop in case fatality rate we'll see shortly aligns closely with this timeline.


SLIDE 7 — Hospital and ICU occupancy
--------------------------------------
Hospital data is more reliable than case counts — it doesn't depend on testing
capacity. ICU occupancy was critically strained in the first and third waves.
ICU and hospital bed peaks don't always coincide — different variants affected
severity differently.


SLIDE 8 — Stringency index vs cases
--------------------------------------
This overlays the Oxford Stringency Index on the case curve. Stringency spikes
tend to precede case drops by about two to three weeks — consistent with the
incubation and reporting delay. Spain's March 2020 estado de alarma was one of
the strictest lockdowns in Europe.


SLIDE 9 — Mobility during lockdown
-------------------------------------
Spain's March 2020 lockdown caused roughly a 60% drop in retail and transit
activity. Later waves caused much smaller drops even with high case counts —
pandemic fatigue and behavioral adaptation. The contrast between wave one and
subsequent waves is striking.


SLIDE 10 — Regional burden by wave (choropleth)
------------------------------------------------
Spain's 17 autonomous communities were hit very differently. Madrid was almost
the sole epicenter in the first wave. By the second and third waves spread became
more uniform. The Balearics and Canaries had low transmission simply because
tourism had stopped and they had smaller resident populations.


SLIDE 11 — Age-sex mortality pyramid
--------------------------------------
COVID mortality was heavily age-skewed. The 80+ group accounts for the vast
majority of deaths — bars below 60 are very narrow. There is also a consistent
male skew across most age groups, seen in other countries too.


SLIDE 12 — Variant prevalence
------------------------------
Alpha drove the third wave in spring 2021, Delta replaced it by summer, and
Omicron became dominant in January 2022. Omicron caused a massive case spike
but with much lower severity per infection — which is why the mortality curve
didn't follow the cases curve.


SLIDE 13 — Tourism collapse
----------------------------
International arrivals fell 66% in 2020 — from 135 million to 46 million.
Tourism is around 12% of Spain's GDP. Poland's economy is much less exposed,
which partly explains why Spain's economic shock was larger and more prolonged.


SLIDE 14 — Unemployment Spain vs Poland
-----------------------------------------
Spain entered the pandemic at 14% unemployment — three times Poland's rate.
It peaked at 16.5% in 2020. Spain's ERTE furlough scheme kept people technically
employed and cushioned the headline number. Poland's labor market was more
resilient due to lower services exposure.


SLIDE 15 — Prophet: train 2020–2022, predict 2023–2024
------------------------------------------------------
We trained a Prophet model on 2020-2022 and predicted 2023-2024. The model
overestimates — it learned the pandemic wave pattern and extrapolated it, without
knowing the virus was transitioning to lower-intensity endemic circulation. This is
a feature, not a bug: it shows the model is doing what it's supposed to, given what
it was shown. We use Prophet rather than SARIMA because Prophet's logistic growth
constraint prevents physically impossible negative forecasts.


SLIDE 16 — Prophet: forecast sensitivity to training window
------------------------------------------------------------
Retraining on the full 2020-2024 data gives a substantially lower 2025 forecast.
The gap between the two models shows how sensitive predictions are to the training
window — the decline phase is load-bearing information. This is the core
methodological point of our inferential section.


SLIDE 17 — Effective reproduction number R(t)
----------------------------------------------
R above one means the epidemic is growing, below one it's declining. The drops
align with lockdown introductions and vaccination reaching critical mass. Omicron
shows a very high initial R — reflecting high transmissibility — but it comes down
quickly, consistent with immunity from prior infection and vaccination.


SLIDE 18 — Case fatality rate by wave
---------------------------------------
First-wave CFR was high due to limited testing and overwhelmed hospitals. It
dropped sharply from mid-2021 with vaccination. The Omicron-era CFR is a fraction
of the first wave's. Spain and Poland follow the same pattern — suggesting it's
a virus-level effect, not country-specific policy.


SLIDE 19 — Key Takeaways
-------------------------
Spain had sharp early waves, strong regional heterogeneity, and an economy
unusually exposed to the tourism shock. Official counts understate the real toll —
excess mortality and positivity data are essential context. Vaccination clearly
reduced mortality, though Omicron's lower intrinsic severity is a confound.
And the Prophet training window substantially changes the forecast — that's
the core methodological point. Thank you.
