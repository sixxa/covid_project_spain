COVID-19 in Spain vs Poland

Data Analysis and Visualization (DAV), 2026

Structure: descriptive trends → regional heterogeneity → socio-economics → inferential models

Daily cases

Spain's waves are sharper and earlier than Poland's; the first wave struck Spain weeks before Poland's.

Daily deaths

Spain's first-wave mortality peak (April 2020) was one of the highest in Europe; vertical lines mark key policy events.

Excess mortality vs reported deaths

Excess mortality significantly exceeded official COVID deaths in early 2020, suggesting substantial under-counting in care homes and at-home deaths.

Test positivity rate

Positivity above 5% (WHO threshold) signals insufficient testing — Spain hit 12.5%+ in spring 2020, meaning official case counts are a severe underestimate for that period.

Vaccination doses per 100 (animated)

Spain rolled out faster than Poland and reached higher coverage; press play to watch monthly progression. Spain peaked near 183 doses per 100 people.

Hospital and ICU occupancy — Spain

ICU capacity was critically strained during the first and third waves; hospital beds peaked later than ICU. Hospital data is more reliable than case counts since it doesn't depend on testing capacity.

Stringency index vs cases

Stringency spikes precede case drops by ~2–3 weeks — consistent with a 14-day incubation and reporting lag. Spain's March 2020 lockdown (estado de alarma) was one of Europe's strictest.

Mobility during lockdown

Spain's March 2020 lockdown caused a ~60% drop in retail and transit mobility. Later waves caused much smaller drops — pandemic fatigue and behavioral adaptation.

Regional burden by wave (animated)

Madrid and Catalonia led the first two waves; later waves spread more uniformly across all 17 autonomous communities. Balearics and Canaries had low transmission once tourism stopped.

Age-sex mortality pyramid

COVID mortality was strongly age-skewed: the 80+ group accounts for the vast majority of deaths. There is also a consistent male skew across all age groups.

Variant prevalence

Alpha dominated spring 2021, Delta replaced it by summer, and Omicron became dominant from January 2022. Omicron caused a massive case spike but with much lower severity per infection.

Tourism collapse

International arrivals fell 66% in 2020 (135M → 46M). Tourism accounts for ~12% of Spain's GDP, amplifying the economic shock far beyond Poland's exposure.

Unemployment: Spain vs Poland

Spain's pre-pandemic unemployment (~14%) was already 3× Poland's rate; it peaked at 16.5% in 2020. Spain's ERTE furlough scheme kept people technically employed and cushioned the headline rise.

SARIMA: train 2020–2022, predict 2023–2024

Model trained before the endemic phase overestimates the endemic baseline — it learned the pandemic wave pattern without knowing the virus would transition to lower-intensity circulation.

SARIMA: forecast sensitivity to training window

Including 2023–2024 data in training substantially lowers the 2025 forecast. The gap between models shows how sensitive predictions are to the training window — the decline phase is load-bearing information.

Effective reproduction number R(t)

R > 1 = growing epidemic; R < 1 = declining. Key transitions align with lockdown introductions and variant switches. Omicron shows a very high initial R — high transmissibility — but it falls quickly.

Case fatality rate by wave

CFR fell sharply after mass vaccination began (mid-2021) and again with Omicron's arrival. Spain and Poland follow the same pattern — a virus-level effect, not country-specific.

Key Takeaways

  • Spain's waves were sharper and regionally heterogeneous — Madrid and Catalonia were hit earliest and hardest.
  • Excess mortality reveals significant under-counting; high test positivity compounds the uncertainty.
  • Vaccination reduced CFR; SARIMA forecast is highly sensitive to whether the decline phase is included in training.
  • Spain's tourism-dependent economy (~12% of GDP) amplified the socio-economic shock relative to Poland.
